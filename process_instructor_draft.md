Purpose

You already know the BS in electrical engineering curriculum at UW Bothell covers circuits, digital logic, and signals, and you know that passing those courses is not enough to land an embedded systems engineer role after graduation. What do you do outside the classroom is what separates the graduates who get hired from the ones who keep applying. This guide covers the best practices that will build their skills, projects, and connections embedded systems employers look for. Use the figure as a road map, then read each section for details.

 


Building firsthand microcontroller experience
embedded rules exist because someone must write the code that runs directly on hardware. Coursework teaches you the theory of how microcontrollers work, but recruiters want to see that you have held the board while the sensor   deployed the project when the lab manual was not there to help you [1].
Start building personal projects on real hardware during fresh menu, not senior year. Start small and expand as your skills grow.

Choosing a starting platform

each microcontroller perform Teaches different skills, and picking one that matches your goals saves months of wasted time on tools you will not use again.
Choose a beginner friendly platform and stick with it for at least three projects. Arduino Is the fastest way to get to know something working, the ESP32 adds Wi-Fi and Bluetooth for connected projects, and the STM32 gets you closer to what professional embedded engineers use daily [2].


Setting up a personal lab bench

waiting for open lab hours limits how often you can work on projects, and serious embedded engineers maintain some form of home workspace.
Set up a small bench at home with a breadboard, jumper wires, multimeter, and soldering iron, and a USB logic analyzer. The full care costs under $200 and pays for itself the first time you debug a single at midnight before a deadline.

Developing C and C++ skills beyond coursework
UW Bothell coursework covers C and C++ at a level that satisfies the curriculum, but embedded roles expect fluency with pointers, memory management, and bare metal firmware that most classes do not teach [2].
Right from there for your own projects using only the hardware references manual in the compiler, without an Arduino library during the work for you. Then move on to small RTOS projects using free RTOS tolerant task scheduling and interpreting handling.

Documenting projects in a public GitHub portfolio

recruiters for embedded roles check GitHub before they call you. A clean, well-documented portfolio proves you can ship working code and explain it to other engineers, which matters more than any single class grade [3].

Publish every personal project to get help with a clean readme that includes a description, a writing diagram, a photo of the work of the hardware, and instructions for building code. Record a short demo video and link it to the top of each readme.

Joining technical communities
embedded hiring runs on referrals and reputation. Engineers at the company you want to work for are already talking to other engineers, and being a part of those conversations gets your name in front of hiring managers before a job posts.
Join the UW parcel IEEE student branch during your sophomore year and attend at least one hackathon or embedded meet up per quarter. Ask questions, share your projects, and follow up with people afterwards on LinkedIn [4].

Landing an embedded focused internship
Generic engineering internships will not prepare you for an embedded role the way your focused one will. An embedded internship gives you production code experience, a real mentor, and a reference who could vouch for your work.
Apply to Seattle area companies that hire embedded interns specifically, such as Boeing, Amazon, and SpaceX [2]. Tailor your resume to highlight firmware projects, link your GitHub in the header, and reference a specific product or team in each cover letter.
